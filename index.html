PMBOK 6 — Integration Management

7 Processes

  • Develop Project Charter (I) — formally authorizes project; key output: Project Charter
  • Develop Project Management Plan (P) — integrates all subsidiary plans; output: PM Plan
  • Direct & Manage Project Work (E) — lead/perform project work; output: Deliverables, Work Performance Data, Change Requests
  • Manage Project Knowledge (E) — use/create knowledge; output: Lessons Learned Register
  • Monitor & Control Project Work (M&C) — track, review, report; output: Work Performance Reports, Change Requests
  • Perform Integrated Change Control (M&C) — review all change requests; key tool: Change Control Board (CCB)
  • Close Project or Phase (C) — finalize project; output: Final Product/Service/Result, Lessons Learned

Key Concepts

Project Charter — authorizes PM to apply resources; includes high-level requirements, milestones, stakeholders, budget, sponsor signature

PM Plan — how the project will be executed, monitored, and controlled. Most comprehensive document

Change Control — all changes go through CCB; approved changes update baselines and plans

Configuration Management — ensures integrity of project documents and deliverables

PMBOK 6 — Scope Management

6 Processes (4 Planning, 1 Executing, 2 M&C)

  • Plan Scope Management (P) — scope management plan, requirements management plan
  • Collect Requirements (P) — interviews, focus groups, surveys, prototypes; output: Requirements Documentation, Traceability Matrix
  • Define Scope (P) — detailed project and product scope; output: Project Scope Statement
  • Create WBS (P) — decompose deliverables; output: WBS, WBS Dictionary, Scope Baseline
  • Validate Scope (E) — formal acceptance of deliverables by customer
  • Control Scope (M&C) — monitor scope status, manage changes to scope baseline

Key Concepts

Product Scope — features/functions of the product

Project Scope — work to deliver the product

Scope Baseline = Project Scope Statement + WBS + WBS Dictionary

WBS — deliverable-oriented hierarchical decomposition; lowest level = Work Package

Gold Plating — adding unrequested features; avoid!

Scope Creep — uncontrolled growth to scope

Requirements Traceability Matrix — links requirements from origin through delivery

PMBOK 6 — Schedule Management

6 Planning + 1 Executing + 1 M&C Processes

  • Plan Schedule Management (P)
  • Define Activities (P) — decompose work packages into schedule activities
  • Sequence Activities (P) — PDM (Finish-to-Start, Start-to-Start, Finish-to-Finish, Start-to-Finish)
  • Estimate Activity Durations (P) — analogous, parametric, three-point (PERT), bottom-up
  • Develop Schedule (P) — critical path, critical chain, resource optimization, schedule compression
  • Control Schedule (M&C) — SPI, SV, schedule forecasts

Critical Path Method

Critical Path — longest path through network; determines project duration; zero float

Float/Slack = LS − ES = LF − EF

Free Float — delay without delaying successor's early start

Total Float — delay without delaying project end date

Schedule Compression

Crashing — add resources to reduce duration; increases cost

Fast Tracking — overlap sequential activities; increases risk

PERT / Three-Point Estimate

E = (O + 4M + P) / 6    SD = (P − O) / 6

Lead & Lag

Lead — acceleration (negative lag); Lag — intentional delay

PMBOK 6 — Quality Management

3 Processes (1P, 1E, 1 M&C)

  • Plan Quality Management (P) — quality standards, metrics, acceptance criteria; output: Quality Management Plan, Quality Metrics
  • Manage Quality (E) — audit quality, improve processes; output: Quality Reports, Test & Evaluation Documents
  • Control Quality (M&C) — verify deliverables meet standards; tools: inspection, testing

Key Concepts & Tools

Quality vs Grade — quality = degree of excellence (conformance); grade = category (different requirements)

Prevention vs Inspection — quality is planned, not inspected in

Cost of Quality (CoQ) = Cost of Conformance + Cost of Nonconformance

7 Basic Quality Tools: Cause-effect (fishbone/Ishikawa), Control charts, Flowcharts, Histogram, Pareto chart, Run chart, Scatter diagram

Control Chart — 3σ rule: Upper/Lower Control Limits at ±3σ; Rule of Seven = 7 consecutive points on one side

Pareto Principle — 80% of problems from 20% of causes

PMBOK 6 — Resource Management

6 Processes (2P, 3E, 1 M&C)

  • Plan Resource Management (P) — RACI, resource management plan
  • Estimate Activity Resources (P) — resource types/quantities
  • Acquire Resources (E) — obtain team members & physical resources
  • Develop Team (E) — build competencies, team interaction, motivation
  • Manage Team (E) — track performance, resolve conflict, manage changes
  • Control Resources (M&C) — ensure resources available as planned

Motivation Theories

Maslow's Hierarchy: Physiological → Safety → Social → Esteem → Self-Actualization

Herzberg: Hygiene factors (prevent dissatisfaction) vs. Motivators (cause satisfaction)

McGregor: Theory X (people dislike work, need control) vs. Theory Y (people self-motivated)

Tuckman's Stages: Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing → Adjourning

Conflict Resolution (best to worst for PMP)

Collaborate/Problem-Solve > Compromise/Reconcile > Smooth/Accommodate > Force/Direct > Withdraw/Avoid

Leadership Styles

Servant Leader · Transformational · Transactional · Laissez-faire

  • Monitor Communications (M&C) — ensure information needs of stakeholders met

Key Concepts

Communication Types: Formal written (reports, contracts) · Formal verbal (presentations) · Informal written (email) · Informal verbal (meetings)

PMBOK 6 — Risk Management

7 Processes (5P, 1E, 1 M&C)

  • Plan Risk Management (P) — risk management plan, risk appetite/tolerance/threshold
  • Identify Risks (P) — Risk Register; tools: brainstorming, checklists, SWOT, Delphi
  • Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis (P) — probability/impact matrix; prioritize risks
  • Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis (P) — numerical analysis; EMV, Monte Carlo, decision trees
  • Plan Risk Responses (P) — response strategies; output: updated Risk Register
  • Implement Risk Responses (E) — execute agreed risk response plans
  • Monitor Risks (M&C) — track identified risks, identify new risks

Risk Response Strategies

Threats: Avoid · Transfer (insure/contract) · Mitigate · Accept (active/passive)

Opportunities: Exploit · Share · Enhance · Accept

Key Terms

Risk Appetite — degree of uncertainty willing to accept

Residual Risk — risk remaining after response; Secondary Risk — new risk created by response

Contingency Reserve — for known-unknown risks; Management Reserve — for unknown-unknowns

EMV = Probability × Impact

Module 4 — Scheduling

OSC 6660 Focus

Network diagrams, critical path, and float calculations. This module has the most math on the exam — forward/backward pass, float, PERT. Prof. Wilson spends significant time here because these appear in almost every PMP exam.

CPM Calculation Steps

  1. Forward Pass: ES → EF (left to right)
  2. Backward Pass: LF → LS (right to left)
  3. Float = LS − ES = LF − EF
  4. Critical Path = all activities with Float = 0
PERT = (O + 4M + P) / 6  ·  SD = (P−O)/6

Crash = add cost, reduce time. Fast Track = overlap tasks, add risk.

Exam Highlights

  • FS = most common dependency type
  • Free Float ≠ Total Float — know the difference
  • Adding float to a non-critical task doesn't shorten the project
  • Resource Leveling may extend the schedule
  • Resource Smoothing works within existing float — no extension
  • Mandatory dependency = hard logic (external or regulatory)

Module 6 — Risk Management

OSC 6660 Focus

Proactive risk identification, analysis, and response planning. The PMP heavily tests the sequence of risk processes and the ability to choose the right response strategy. Prof. Wilson emphasizes that risk management is ongoing — not a one-time event.

Risk Process Sequence

  1. Plan Risk Management
  2. Identify Risks → Risk Register
  3. Qualitative Analysis (P×I matrix)
  4. Quantitative Analysis (EMV, Monte Carlo)
  5. Plan Risk Responses
  6. Implement Risk Responses
  7. Monitor Risks (ongoing)

Exam Highlights

  • Contingency = known-unknowns (in baseline)
  • Management Reserve = unknown-unknowns (NOT in baseline)
  • Avoid = eliminate threat entirely
  • Transfer = insurance or contract (impact shifts, not risk)
  • Secondary risk = created BY your response
  • Residual risk = what remains AFTER response
  • Workaround = unplanned response after risk occurs

Module 7 — Quality Management

OSC 6660 Focus

PMI's quality philosophy: prevention over inspection, quality is planned and built in — not tested in afterward. Covers the 7 basic quality tools and how to tell them apart on the exam.

7 Basic Quality Tools

  • Fishbone (Ishikawa) — root cause analysis (6 Ms)
  • Control Chart — statistical process control; Rule of 7
  • Pareto Chart — 80/20 rule; prioritize vital few
  • Histogram — frequency distribution of one variable
  • Scatter Diagram — correlation between two variables
  • Flowchart — process flow and decision points
  • Run Chart — performance trend over time

Exam Highlights

  • Manage Quality (E) = audit processes. Control Quality (M&C) = inspect products
  • CoQ: Conformance (prevention + appraisal) vs. Non-Conformance (rework, warranty)
  • Quality ≠ Grade. Low quality is always a problem; low grade may not be
  • Control chart: UCL/LCL at ±3σ. 7 consecutive points = out of control
  • Gold plating is a quality problem (adds untested scope)

Six Sigma, DMAIC & DMADV

What is Six Sigma?

Six Sigma is a data-driven methodology for eliminating defects and reducing process variation. The goal is 3.4 defects per million opportunities (DPMO) — 99.99966% defect-free.

Core Belief: Continuous efforts to reduce variation in process outputs are key to business success. Quality is measured statistically.

Founder: Motorola (1980s); popularized by GE (Jack Welch)

Two methodologies: DMAIC (improve existing) and DMADV (design new)

Belt Levels

  • White Belt — Basic awareness
  • Yellow Belt — Project team member support
  • Green Belt — Part-time project leader
  • Black Belt — Full-time project leader
  • Master Black Belt — Coach/mentor, strategic deployment
  • Champion — Executive sponsor; removes obstacles

DMAIC vs DMADV — When to Use Which

Use DMAIC when…

  • A process already exists
  • Performance is below target
  • Root cause is unknown
  • Customer is dissatisfied with an existing product

Goal: Fix and optimize existing

Use DMADV when…

  • No process exists yet
  • Existing process is beyond repair
  • Designing a new product from scratch
  • Radical performance improvement is needed

Goal: Build new to Six Sigma standards

Customer needs, expectations, preferences captured through surveys, interviews, focus groups.

Process Capability Indices

Poka-Yoke (Mistake-Proofing)

Design mechanisms that prevent errors from occurring or make errors immediately obvious (e.g., USB-A only fits one way).

FMEA (Failure Mode & Effects Analysis)

Proactively identifies potential failure modes, their effects, and risk priority. RPN = Severity × Occurrence × Detection.

5 Whys

Ask "Why?" repeatedly (typically 5 times) to drill down from a symptom to the root cause of a problem.

The 5 Scrum Events

🏃

Sprint (1–4 weeks, consistent length)

The container for all other events. A fixed-length iteration producing a "Done," usable increment. No changes that endanger the Sprint Goal are made during the Sprint. Only the Product Owner can cancel a Sprint.

📋

Sprint Planning (Max 8 hrs for 4-week Sprint)

Entire Scrum Team defines the Sprint Goal and selects backlog items. Development Team forecasts what can be delivered and creates the Sprint Backlog. Answers: Why? What? How?

☀️

Daily Scrum (15 minutes, same time/place, Dev Team only)

Inspect progress toward Sprint Goal and adapt Sprint Backlog. Three questions: What did I do yesterday? What will I do today? Any impediments? Scrum Master facilitates but does not lead.

🎯

Sprint Review (Max 4 hrs for 4-week Sprint; includes stakeholders)

Scrum Team + stakeholders inspect the Increment and adapt the Product Backlog. This is NOT a status meeting — it is a working session. Product Owner explains what is Done. Team demos the work.

🔄

Sprint Retrospective (Max 3 hrs for 4-week Sprint; team only)

Inspect how the last Sprint went regarding people, relationships, processes, and tools. Identify improvements. The Retro is about the process; the Review is about the product. Always occurs after Review, before next Planning.

🧭 Ch 3: PM Principles

12 Principles — The "Why" Behind Every PM Decision

These principles from The Standard guide how a PM thinks and behaves. They underpin all 7 Performance Domains. They are not prescriptive rules — they require professional judgment.

1Stewardship 🛡

Be a diligent, respectful, caring steward. Act in the best interests of all stakeholders. Uphold PMI's Code of Ethics.

Exam: When in doubt, the ethical/transparent choice is almost always correct.

2Team 👥

Create a collaborative project team environment. Recognize individual contributions. Foster psychological safety.

Exam: PMI favors servant leadership — enable the team, don't micromanage.

3Stakeholders 🤝

Effectively engage with stakeholders. Understand their interests, needs, and expectations proactively.

Exam: Engage stakeholders early and continuously — don't wait for problems.

4Value 💎

Continuously evaluate whether the project is delivering intended value. Pivot if it isn't.

Exam: If a project no longer delivers value, escalate — don't blindly continue.

5Systems Thinking 🔗

Recognize system interactions — changes in one area ripple through others. Projects are part of larger systems.

Exam: Consider downstream impacts and org context before deciding.

6Leadership 🌟

Demonstrate leadership appropriate to the situation. Leadership ≠ authority. PMI favors situational and transformational leadership.

Exam: Lead by example and inspire — not by command.

7Tailoring ✂️

Adapt processes, tools, and methods to fit the project's unique context. No single approach works everywhere.

Exam: Always pick the answer that fits the specific context described.

8Quality ✅

Build quality into processes AND deliverables. Prevention is cheaper than inspection. Cost of quality = conformance + nonconformance.

Exam: Quality is planned in — not inspected in at the end.

9Complexity 🌀

Navigate complexity from human behavior, ambiguity, system interactions, and technological uncertainty. Use iterative approaches for high-complexity environments.

Exam: Adaptive/agile preferred when requirements are unclear or rapidly changing.

10Risk ⚠️

Optimize risk responses. Proactively identify threats AND opportunities. Responses should be proportional to the risk's significance.

Exam: Opportunities are risks too — always consider both sides.

11Adaptability & Resiliency 🔄

Build the ability to respond to change without panic. Bounce forward from setbacks. Embed flexibility into plans.

Exam: Agile and hybrid approaches embody this principle most directly.

12Change 🚀

Enable change to achieve the envisioned future state. A delivered project nobody adopts is a failure. PM must facilitate organizational adoption.

Exam: Benefits realization requires change management, not just delivery.

🔁 Ch 4: Project Life Cycles

Three Development Approaches

🔵 Predictive (Waterfall)

Full scope defined upfront. Sequential phases. Low change tolerance. Best for stable, well-understood requirements.

Examples: Construction, infrastructure, regulated industries

🟢 Adaptive (Agile)

Iterative/incremental delivery. Scope evolves. High change tolerance. Best for innovative products with evolving requirements.

Examples: Software, digital products, R&D

🟡 Hybrid

Combines both. Predictive for governance & contracts; adaptive for delivery. Most real-world projects.

Examples: Large IT programs, organizational change initiatives

The 5 Focus Areas (Process Groups — Restored in PMBOK 8th)

🚀 Initiating — Authorize the project. Develop Project Charter. Identify key stakeholders. Define high-level objectives. (OSC M1, M2)
📋 Planning — Develop all subsidiary plans. Establish scope, schedule, cost, quality, resource, communications, risk, procurement, and stakeholder engagement baselines. (OSC M3–M6)
⚙️ Executing — Carry out the plan. Manage team, stakeholders, and vendors. Produce deliverables. Issue change requests as needed. (OSC M7–M9)

🏁 Closing — Finalize deliverables. Get formal acceptance. Release resources. Document lessons learned. Archive records. (OSC M10)

📅 Schedule Domain

Network Diagrams & Dependencies

PDM (Precedence Diagramming Method) — 4 dependency types:

Finish-to-Start (FS)
Most common. B can't start until A finishes.
Start-to-Start (SS)
B can't start until A starts.
Finish-to-Finish (FF)
B can't finish until A finishes.
Start-to-Finish (SF)
Rare. B can't finish until A starts.

Lead: overlap (negative lag) | Lag: delay | Mandatory: hard logic | Discretionary: soft/preferred logic

Critical Path Method (CPM)

Critical Path = longest path through the network = project duration. Activities on the critical path have zero float.

Float = LS − ES = LF − EF
Free Float = ES(next) − EF(current)

Schedule Compression:

TechniqueHowRisk
CrashingAdd resources to critical path↑ Cost
Fast-trackingOverlap sequential activities↑ Rework risk

Conflict Resolution (Best → Last Resort)

1. Collaborate / Problem Solve — Work together for win-win. PMI's preferred approach.
2. Compromise — Both parties give something up. Neither fully wins.
3. Accommodate / Smooth — Emphasize agreement, downplay differences. Short-term only.
4. Force / Direct — PM uses authority to decide. Use sparingly — kills morale.
5. Withdraw / Avoid — Postpone or sidestep. Only for truly minor conflicts.

⚖️ Ethics & Standards

Why This Matters on the PMP

The PMP exam includes 5–10 direct ethics questions every sitting — plus dozens of situational questions with an ethical dimension. The PMI Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct has 4 core values, each with mandatory (what you must do) and aspirational (what you should do) standards.

🔵 Responsibility

Aspirational: Make decisions based on best interests of society, safety, and the environment. Accept accountability for our actions and their consequences.

Mandatory: Report unethical or illegal conduct. Disclose conflicts of interest. Do not make false or misleading claims. Protect proprietary or confidential information.

EXAM SIGNAL:

When a sponsor asks you to hide information or skip a process — you must refuse and report.

🔵 Respect

Aspirational: Show high regard for ourselves, others, and the resources entrusted to us. Listen first, seek to understand cultural differences, and approach others with empathy.

Mandatory: Do not use your position to influence others for personal gain. Do not act abusively toward others. Respect intellectual property rights.

EXAM SIGNAL:

When team members have a cultural conflict — seek to understand first, don't impose your norms.

🔵 Fairness

Aspirational: Make decisions impartially and objectively. Proactively examine our own biases and avoid discriminatory practices.

Mandatory: Disclose conflicts of interest. Refuse gifts or benefits that could influence your decisions. Do not favor someone for personal gain. Provide equal access to information.

EXAM SIGNAL:

If a vendor gives you a gift before contract award — you must disclose it and recuse yourself.

🔵 Honesty

Aspirational: Seek to understand the truth and act in a truthful manner. Create an environment of transparency and candor on the project.

Mandatory: Do not engage in or condone deception. Do not make dishonest statements to gain business advantage. Report accurate project status even when it's bad news.

EXAM SIGNAL:

Never falsify status reports or sign off on work you know is incomplete — even under pressure.

How PMI Thinks — Exam Strategy

The Single Most Important Rule

PMI's "right answer" is always the one a perfect, by-the-book, proactive PM would choose — not what's fastest, cheapest, or most realistic.

If you're choosing between "follow the process" and "skip the process to save time," PMI always wants you to follow the process.

PMI's Mental Model

  1. Proactive over reactive — Plan for risks before they happen
  2. Process-driven — There's a right step for everything
  3. Stakeholder-first — Communicate and engage constantly
  4. Data-driven decisions — Analyze before acting
  5. Ethical always — No exceptions, no shortcuts
  6. Team empowerment — Servant leadership, remove impediments
  7. Change control is sacred — Nothing changes without a CCB
  8. Document everything — Lessons learned, change logs, registers

How to Eliminate Wrong Answers

🚫 Eliminate These Answer Patterns

  • Anything that bypasses the change control board
  • Skipping documentation to save time
  • Making a unilateral decision without consulting stakeholders
  • Blaming team members or vendors without investigation
  • Accepting scope changes verbally without a formal process
  • Promising a delivery date without analysis
  • Doing nothing / monitoring without acting

✅ These Are Usually Right

  • Communicate with stakeholders first
  • Consult the PM plan / risk register / lessons learned
  • Submit a formal change request through the CCB
  • Perform impact analysis before deciding
  • Proactively identify and address risks
  • Follow the process even if it takes longer
  • Escalate appropriately after attempting resolution

Common Trap Answers — PMI vs Reality

🪤 Traps PMI Sets

  • "The sponsor said it's OK" → Not good enough — still needs formal change request
  • "Add it to prevent rework later" → Gold plating — always wrong
  • "It's a small change" → Still needs CCB approval
  • "The team can figure it out" → PM must resolve, not delegate conflict
  • "You've handled this before" → Check the plan first anyway
  • "To save time, skip documentation" → Never correct on PMP

🧠 PMI Mindset Reminders

  • The PM is proactive, not reactive
  • Agile PMs are servant leaders, not commanders
  • The PM always communicates bad news early
  • Lessons learned are documented throughout, not just at close
  • The PM's job is to follow the plan, not make unilateral decisions
  • In agile: the team self-organizes — PM facilitates, not assigns

💡 Power Skills (formerly Leadership)

The "people" side of project management. These skills are the #1 differentiator between average and exceptional PMs.

Leadership Styles (tested)

  • Servant Leadership — Remove impediments, enable team (Agile default)
  • Transformational — Inspire change, high vision
  • Transactional — Reward/punish based on performance
  • Laissez-Faire — Hands-off (only works with expert teams)
  • Directive — Tell team exactly what to do (crisis situations)

Emotional Intelligence (Goleman)

  • Self-Awareness — Know your own emotions
  • Self-Regulation — Manage your reactions
  • Motivation — Internal drive, resilience
  • Empathy — Understand others' emotions
  • Social Skills — Build relationships, influence

Conflict Resolution Order (PMI)

  1. Collaborate / Problem-Solve (BEST)
  2. Compromise
  3. Accommodate / Smooth
  4. Avoid / Withdraw
  5. Force (WORST)

📈 Business Acumen

PMs must understand the organizational and business context of their projects — not just the technical deliverables.

Key Concepts

  • Business Case — Justifies the project; why we're doing it
  • Benefits Realization — Ensuring project benefits are actually delivered post-project
  • Benefits Management Plan — Defines how/when benefits will be measured
  • Portfolio/Program alignment — Project contributes to strategic goals
  • NPV / ROI / Payback Period — Financial selection criteria
  • Opportunity Cost — Value of the next best alternative

Org Context & Governance

  • PMO types — Supportive, Controlling, Directive
  • Org structures — Functional, Matrix, Projectized
  • EEFs vs OPAs — External inputs vs internal assets
  • Governance — Who approves what, escalation paths
  • Compliance requirements — Legal, regulatory, industry standards
PMP Tip: If benefits realization extends beyond project close, it's the program/portfolio or operations that manage it — not the PM.
